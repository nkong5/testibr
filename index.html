Expectations of Iranians on responsible business: opportunities and challenges

As European companies look to invest in Iran, they will have to live up to the expectations of international standards on responsible business such as the OECD Guidelines on Multinational Enterprises that apply everywhere an OECD based company operates, or the IFC Performance Standards if they look for financial support. But is the pressure on business to act responsibly in Iran only based on international standards? Is it a "western" discussion? Or do Iranians themselves expect business to act responsibly when investing in Iran?

In order to rely on hard data, these are some of the questions that the Iran Business Responsibility project) (IBR) addresses in an exclusive groundbreaking poll it carried out inside Iran to measure the attitudes of urban Iranians toward responsible business issues. The results are significant: Iranians are keenly aware of business' responsibility to ensure both the environment and people are respected in the context of business activities.

The results show not only that a large majority of Iranians are familiar with the term "responsible business" but that they associate the term with many of the same issues that are reflected in international standards on responsible business. For example, a large percentage of Iranians feel strongly that business is responsible for protecting the environment, ensuring occupational health and safety and respecting workers' rights. They also believe business is responsible for ensuring the company's activities do not harm people and for respecting consumer rights.

The poll also showed that Iranians consider the reputation of the company and the absence of harassment in the workplace among the most important factors when considering employment - after such fundamental factors as salary and liking the job.

When asked about non-discrimination in access to employment, Iranian respondents showed an active agreement with the idea that laws should ensure people with disabilities do not suffer discrimination. The poll, on the other hand, highlights areas where European companies may have some challenges when implementing other types of non-discrimination policies.

The poll showed a strong bias in favour of trusting Iranian state-owned companies to be responsible, while European companies were much less likely to be trusted to be responsible - especially in key responsible business issues like ensuring non-harassment at work, occupational health and safety, respecting the environment and considering the public interest when making business decisions. This also demonstrated that European companies will have to prove themselves in terms of acting responsibly and could gain a competitive advantage in doing so.

In sum, this unique poll confirms that while European companies must comply with international standards when operating in Iran, Iranians themselves also have expectations of companies when it comes to respecting people and the environment, and these expectations will impact any business activity in the Iranian context.
